The Star That Wasn’t

When I was a young fellow in college, one evening, I went to an abandoned old water tower on campus, and climbed up the rusty ladder on its inside. Through a hole in the roof I emerged onto the top, and sat myself on the cold rough concrete. Friction prevented me from sliding off it and falling 120 feet to the ground. The sun had already set by then, but there was still quite a bit of light. In the west, amid the orange and the purple I saw, floating majestically over the horizon, a silvery-yellow jewel of a star. I felt like an ancient mariner who had clambered up the mast of his ship to read the sea, except that the sea wasn’t the ground below me but space itself, through which I could feel the earth move. Maybe if you are perched high enough, you can feel the earth spin. Or maybe it was just vertigo.

I did not know it then, but many people had seen and been stunned by the same sight as I, for at least the past five thousand years, perhaps more. The star I saw was the celebrated evening star. To the ancient Sumerians it was the goddess Ishtar, from which we got the word “star”. Native American civilizations considered the evening star to be male rather than female. It is the Mayan god Quetzalcoatl, on which they based their elaborate calendar according to which the world will come to an end later this year.

The ancients also noticed, about 8 days after the evening star had dropped off, a morning star, equally bright, that shows up just before sunrise on the eastern horizon. It was our second famous star. The Romans called it Lucifer. Lucifer was a falling angel: it shined brightly ahead of the sun for a few dawns, sinking lower and lower until finally it disappeared completely into…​ hell. And then after many weeks, the evening star shows up again after sunset on the west, and the cycle continues.

It took a while before the ancients figured out that the morning and evening stars were the same, and this fed into our resurrection stories. Not only is it not two stars, it is not a star at all, but a planet we now call Venus. It is such a brilliant object in the sky because it is enveloped in reflective clouds which probes have found to be nothing more fancy than battery acid! The reason we see Venus as both evening and morning star is because its orbit is within the Earth’s, so we get to see it glancingly on the twilight horizon both coming and going as it keeps overtaking Earth on the inside track. Between the time that it is the morning star and the evening star, Venus is tucked in directly between Sun and Earth, so we could in principle see it during the day, if only the Sun weren’t so bright.

If circumstances were right, we could see Venus during the day during a total solar eclipse. Even rarer, if the orbits aligned just so, we could see Venus as a black dot coursing across the shining sun. This is called the Transit of Venus and occurs in pairs every 100+ years. Humans have observed only six Venus transits thus far. The next transit is on June 6th, a few short weeks from now. And the transit after that will not be until 2117.

I remember attending a meeting here a few weeks back, when the respected members of the Northborough Historical Society used archived material to describe the life and times in this area around the turn of the 19th century. The snapshots of the people then were both quaint and familiar. They were both vulnerable and courageous, ordinary yet filled with a sense of adventure. We could not help but be amused at their foibles and preoccupations and their technologies, but our amusement was tinged with fellow-feeling. We will be overtaken in our turn by history.

Maybe the people in that presentation saw the Venus transits in the latter half of the 19th century. Barring a medical miracle, all of them would have joined the Great Majority before this June’s transit. And barring a medical miracle, we would have joined them before the transit of 2117. But there is a good chance that in 2117, there will be band of dedicated people at the Northborough Historical Society looking back at us, and maybe they will look kindly on our times, with understanding, and sure, a laugh or two.

Oh yes, I did get down the water tower safely that evening. I had Venus’s battery-acid light to guide me.
