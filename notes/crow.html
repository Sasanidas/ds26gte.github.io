Life of a Crow

Some years ago, a colleague and I were sent by our company to Erie, Pennsylvania, on a site visit. Erie was clearly a large town that had seen better days. The roads were empty but disproportionately huge. We asked a local staff member to suggest a dinner place. He gave the name and address of what he said was a really fancy Italian restaurant. So we went there in our rental car at close of business.

I think having lived in Houston, Austin and the Boston area didn’t quite prepare me for an Italian restaurant in Erie. It was a crumbling building in a poorly lit neighborhood. The waitstaff had several days' growth of beard, and cigarettes hanging limply from their lips — and that was just the women. But they did give us heaping plates of pasta that I didn’t dare ignore. When we had done and rushed out, I found that a crow had spat on the rental’s windshield (and by spat I don’t really mean spat). One would have thought it was a gull, because of the great lake of Erie nearby, but it was indeed a crow because I saw it flying away.

I knew crows. I grew up in Vizag, an oceanside town in India where crows were an infestation. They were everywhere. (I even knew that the collection word for crows was "murder": a murder of crows, an appropriate name given the murderous cacophony they made.) Devout housewives would keep food out for them every morning, because crows were supposed to represent one’s ancestors. If so, I guess it meant that our ancestors had really bad potty-training. There was one particularly aggressive fellow that would never fail to spit on my clothes that were set to dry on the clothesline. I had come to recognize him by a very distinctive white streak below his left eye. Nobody thought much of it, it was just a small dash of guano — I was just expected to spot-wash my clothes and that was that. It was not supposed to be a big deal. But I really didn’t like it.

I started to wonder how long crows lived. I had never seen a dead crow. Dead robins, yes, but never a dead crow. Did they die of old age, their bodies cleared away by a scavenger species? Or were they picked off by a predator while they were still fit? I didn’t know of any natural predators for the crow. They certainly didn’t migrate. Why would they? They had nice warm weather in south India throughout the year.

Still I knew that all living things must die, and it was only a matter of time before my crow bought it. Or so I hoped. But day after day, there he was, causing havoc. And my mom of course wasn’t helping, by leaving him a minor feast of rice and yogurt and curry every morning. I could see him winking wickedly at me as he left a processed version of the food on my drying clothes.

Then one day he didn’t show up. I was happy that my clothes were clean, and didn’t think much of it. There were other crows, but they were less boisterous and didn’t bother me. By afternoon, it was out of my mind.

The next morning I was absently expecting him to be there, before I realized that he was already missing the previous day. Where was my crow? Did he get weak? Was he visiting relatives?

By the third day, I realized something was wrong. The event I had been awaiting had probably happened. I was not nearly as relieved as I’d thought I’d be. Perhaps he fell, and some cat or other creature finished him off?

A few more days of this, and it cemented my conviction that my nemesis was my late nemesis.

I was twelve years old. Suddenly the idea that things that you counted on to continue could one day just stop forever — that idea became a lot less abstract. I started looking at my surroundings, my parents and my siblings, friends and strangers and the world at large, a bit more intently, almost willing them to stay static snapshots. They no doubt thought I was crazy.

Then one Sunday, as I was taking my clothes off the line, I noticed the spittage. I slowly turned to the top of the house wall, and there was the crow, with the distinctive white streak under his left eye. We stared at each other for several seconds. Then he winked at me and took to the sky. After that, I would take care to put out food for him. I’d like to say he reciprocated the gesture by not sullying my clothes, but no, he was a bit of a comedian I guess. But I didn’t mind as much.

Years later, as I emerged from the fanciest Italian restaurant in Erie, Pennsylvania, and cleaned the windshield of my rental, I saw a crow, with a white streak under its left eye, circling above and away.

But that must be an illusion, right?
